## Fourier Series on a Finite Interval

Let $f \in L^2([-L, L])$. By the completeness of the orthonormal basis $\left\{ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2L}} e^{i n\pi x/L} \right\}_{n \in \mathbb{Z}}$, we can write:

$$
f(x) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n \, e^{i n \pi x / L}
$$

where the Fourier coefficients are:

$$
c_n = \frac{1}{2L} \int_{-L}^{L} f(x) \, e^{-i n \pi x / L} \, dx
$$

Define the frequency variable $\xi_n = \frac{n}{2L}$ and spacing $\Delta\xi = \frac{1}{2L}$. Then:

$$
f(x) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(\xi_n) \, e^{2\pi i \xi_n x} \, \Delta\xi
$$

where $\hat{f}(\xi_n) = \int_{-L}^{L} f(x) e^{-2\pi i \xi_n x} \, dx$.

## The Limiting Argument ($L \to \infty$)

As $L \to \infty$, the spacing $\Delta\xi \to 0$ and the sum converges to a Riemann integral:

$$
f(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(\xi) \, e^{2\pi i \xi x} \, d\xi
$$

where the **Fourier Transform** is defined as:

$$
\boxed{\hat{f}(\xi) = \mathcal{F}\{f\}(\xi) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) \, e^{-2\pi i \xi x} \, dx}
$$

This argument is heuristic. The rigorous foundation requires working in $L^1(\mathbb{R}) \cap L^2(\mathbb{R})$ first, then extending to all of $L^2(\mathbb{R})$ via density.

## Plancherel's Theorem

For $f, g \in L^2(\mathbb{R})$, the Fourier transform is an isometric isomorphism:

$$
\langle f, g \rangle_{L^2} = \langle \hat{f}, \hat{g} \rangle_{L^2}
$$

Setting $f = g$, this gives **Parseval's identity**:

$$
\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |f(x)|^2 \, dx = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |\hat{f}(\xi)|^2 \, d\xi
$$

**Proof sketch.** For $f, g \in L^1 \cap L^2$:

$$
\int \hat{f}(\xi) \overline{\hat{g}(\xi)} \, d\xi = \int \hat{f}(\xi) \int g(x) e^{2\pi i \xi x} \, dx \, d\xi
$$

Fubini's theorem (justified by $L^1$ integrability) allows interchange of integration:

$$
= \int g(x) \int \hat{f}(\xi) e^{2\pi i \xi x} \, d\xi \, dx = \int g(x) f(x) \, dx
$$

The extension to all of $L^2$ follows from the density of $L^1 \cap L^2$ in $L^2$. $\square$

## The Inversion Formula

**Theorem.** If $f \in L^1(\mathbb{R})$ and $\hat{f} \in L^1(\mathbb{R})$, then for a.e. $x$:

$$
f(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(\xi) \, e^{2\pi i \xi x} \, d\xi
$$

**Proof.** Define the approximate identity $\phi_\epsilon(x) = e^{-\pi \epsilon^2 x^2}$ (a Gaussian). Its Fourier transform is $\hat{\phi}_\epsilon(\xi) = \epsilon^{-1} e^{-\pi \xi^2 / \epsilon^2}$. Consider:

$$
\int \hat{f}(\xi) \, e^{2\pi i \xi x} \, e^{-\pi \epsilon^2 \xi^2} \, d\xi = \int f(t) \, \phi_\epsilon(x-t) \, dt
$$

The right side is the convolution $f * \phi_\epsilon$, which converges to $f(x)$ at every Lebesgue point as $\epsilon \to 0^+$. Passing the limit under the integral on the left (by dominated convergence, using $\hat{f} \in L^1$) completes the proof. $\square$

## Key Properties

| Property | Statement |
|---|---|
| Linearity | $\mathcal{F}\{\alpha f + \beta g\} = \alpha \hat{f} + \beta \hat{g}$ |
| Translation | $\mathcal{F}\{f(x-a)\}(\xi) = e^{-2\pi i a \xi} \hat{f}(\xi)$ |
| Modulation | $\mathcal{F}\{e^{2\pi i \xi_0 x} f(x)\}(\xi) = \hat{f}(\xi - \xi_0)$ |
| Scaling | $\mathcal{F}\{f(ax)\}(\xi) = \frac{1}{\lvert a \rvert}\hat{f}\!\left(\frac{\xi}{a}\right)$ |
| Convolution | $\mathcal{F}\{f * g\} = \hat{f} \cdot \hat{g}$ |
| Differentiation | $\mathcal{F}\{f'\}(\xi) = 2\pi i \xi \hat{f}(\xi)$ |

## Numerical Computation: DFT Correspondence

The Discrete Fourier Transform approximates the continuous transform on a uniform grid:

```python
import numpy as np

def continuous_ft_approx(
    f_vals: np.ndarray,
    x_vals: np.ndarray,
    xi_vals: np.ndarray,
) -> np.ndarray:
    """
    Naive O(N^2) approximation of the continuous FT.
    For demonstration only — use np.fft.fft in practice.
    """
    dx = x_vals[1] - x_vals[0]
    F = np.array([
        np.sum(f_vals * np.exp(-2j * np.pi * xi * x_vals)) * dx
        for xi in xi_vals
    ])
    return F

# The FFT computes:  F[k] = sum_{n=0}^{N-1} f[n] * exp(-2pi*i*k*n/N)
# With physical frequency resolution:  delta_xi = 1 / (N * dx)

N = 1024
dx = 1e-3  # 1 mm spatial sampling
xi = np.fft.fftfreq(N, d=dx)  # Frequency axis in cycles/m
```

The relationship between DFT and continuous FT becomes exact in the limit $N \to \infty$, $\Delta x \to 0$ with $N \Delta x$ fixed.

## Summary

The Fourier Transform arises naturally from the Fourier series as the period $2L \to \infty$. Rigorously, it is an isometric isomorphism on $L^2(\mathbb{R})$ (Plancherel), and the inversion formula holds for every Lebesgue point when both $f$ and $\hat{f}$ are integrable. These properties — linearity, the convolution theorem, and the differentiation rule — make it the central tool of linear analysis.
