## Starting Point: Fourier Series on $[-L, L]$

Let $f \in L^2([-L,L])$. The family of exponentials $\left\lbrace \frac{1}{\sqrt{2L}} e^{i n \pi x / L} \right\rbrace_{n \in \mathbb{Z}}$ forms a complete orthonormal basis in $L^2([-L,L])$. Orthonormality follows from the integral identity

$$
\frac{1}{2L} \int_{-L}^{L} e^{i(m-n)\pi x / L} \, dx = \delta_{mn},
$$

where $\delta_{mn}$ is the Kronecker delta. By completeness, *every* square-integrable function on $[-L,L]$ admits the representation

$$
f(x) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n \, e^{i n \pi x / L},
$$

with Fourier coefficients

$$
c_n = \frac{1}{2L} \int_{-L}^{L} f(x) \, e^{-i n \pi x / L} \, dx.
$$

Convergence holds in the $L^2$ norm: $\left\| f - \sum_{|n| \le N} c_n e^{in\pi x/L} \right\|_{L^2} \to 0$ as $N \to \infty$. For pointwise convergence at a point $x_0$ one requires additional regularity — Dini's condition or bounded variation in a neighbourhood of $x_0$ suffices.

## Taking the Limit $L \to \infty$

Introduce the discrete frequency variable $\xi_n = \frac{n}{2L}$ and frequency spacing $\Delta\xi = \frac{1}{2L}$. Substituting into the Fourier series gives

$$
f(x) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} \left[ \int_{-L}^{L} f(t) \, e^{-2\pi i \xi_n t} \, dt \right] e^{2\pi i \xi_n x} \, \Delta\xi.
$$

As $L \to \infty$, the spacing $\Delta\xi \to 0$ and the discrete frequencies fill out the real line. Under appropriate regularity on $f$, the Riemann sum converges to an integral:

$$
f(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(\xi) \, e^{2\pi i \xi x} \, d\xi,
$$

where we define the **Fourier Transform**:

$$
\boxed{\hat{f}(\xi) = \mathcal{F}\lbrace f\rbrace(\xi) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x) \, e^{-2\pi i \xi x} \, dx}
$$

and the **Inverse Fourier Transform**:

$$
f(x) = \mathcal{F}^{-1}\lbrace\hat{f}\rbrace(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(\xi) \, e^{2\pi i \xi x} \, d\xi.
$$

> **Warning.** The above limiting argument is heuristic. The passage from sum to integral requires justification via dominated convergence or uniform convergence conditions. A rigorous treatment requires working in the Schwartz space $\mathcal{S}(\mathbb{R})$ of rapidly decreasing functions, where the Fourier transform is a topological isomorphism, before extending to $L^1$ and $L^2$ by density and continuity arguments.

## Rigorous Treatment in $L^1(\mathbb{R})$

For $f \in L^1(\mathbb{R})$, the defining integral converges absolutely:

$$
|\hat{f}(\xi)| \le \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |f(x)| \, dx = \|f\|_{L^1} < \infty.
$$

Thus $\hat{f}$ is bounded and continuous. Furthermore, the **Riemann-Lebesgue Lemma** asserts that $\hat{f}(\xi) \to 0$ as $|\xi| \to \infty$.

**Proof of the Riemann-Lebesgue Lemma.** For step functions the result follows by direct computation of finite sums of sinusoids, each of which vanishes as $|\xi| \to \infty$. Since step functions are dense in $L^1(\mathbb{R})$ and the map $f \mapsto \hat{f}$ is a contraction ($\|\hat{f}\|_\infty \le \|f\|_{L^1}$), the result extends to all of $L^1$ by a standard approximation argument. $\square$

However, $L^1$ is insufficient for the inversion formula. The transform $\hat{f}$ of an $L^1$ function need not itself be in $L^1$, so the inversion integral may not converge. The canonical example is the characteristic function $f = \mathbf{1}_{[-1,1]}$, whose transform is the sinc function $\hat{f}(\xi) = \frac{\sin(2\pi\xi)}{\pi\xi}$, which is not absolutely integrable.

## Extension to $L^2$: The Plancherel Theorem

**Theorem (Plancherel).** There exists a unique unitary operator $\mathcal{F}: L^2(\mathbb{R}) \to L^2(\mathbb{R})$ that agrees with the $L^1$ Fourier transform on $L^1 \cap L^2$. In particular, for all $f \in L^2(\mathbb{R})$:

$$
\|\hat{f}\|_{L^2} = \|f\|_{L^2}.
$$

**Proof sketch.** For $f \in L^1 \cap L^2$, Parseval's identity holds by Fubini's theorem (as shown below). Since $L^1 \cap L^2$ is dense in $L^2$, and $\mathcal{F}$ is an isometry on this dense subspace, it extends uniquely to a bounded linear operator on $L^2$ by the B.L.T. theorem (bounded linear transformation). By Fatou's lemma, the isometry property is preserved in the limit.

For $f, g \in L^1 \cap L^2$:

$$
\int \hat{f}(\xi) \overline{\hat{g}(\xi)} \, d\xi = \int \hat{f}(\xi) \int g(x) e^{2\pi i \xi x} \, dx \, d\xi.
$$

Fubini's theorem (applicable because $f \in L^1$) allows interchange of integration:

$$
= \int g(x) \int \hat{f}(\xi) e^{2\pi i \xi x} \, d\xi \, dx = \int g(x) \overline{f(x)} \, dx = \langle f, g \rangle_{L^2}.
$$

**Polarisation identity.** As a consequence, the Fourier transform preserves the full inner product structure, not just norms:

$$
\langle \hat{f}, \hat{g} \rangle_{L^2} = \langle f, g \rangle_{L^2} \quad \forall f, g \in L^2(\mathbb{R}).
$$

## The Inversion Formula

**Theorem (Pointwise inversion).** If $f \in L^1(\mathbb{R})$ and $\hat{f} \in L^1(\mathbb{R})$, then for almost every $x \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$
f(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(\xi) \, e^{2\pi i \xi x} \, d\xi.
$$

The proof uses an approximate identity argument. Define $\phi_\epsilon(x) = e^{-\pi \epsilon^2 x^2}$. Its Fourier transform is $\hat{\phi}_\epsilon(\xi) = \epsilon^{-1} e^{-\pi \xi^2/\epsilon^2}$. The regularised integral

$$
\int \hat{f}(\xi) e^{2\pi i \xi x} e^{-\pi \epsilon^2 \xi^2} \, d\xi = (f * \phi_\epsilon)(x)
$$

converges to $f(x)$ at every Lebesgue point as $\epsilon \to 0^+$. Passing the limit under the left integral by dominated convergence (justified since $\hat{f} \in L^1$) establishes the result.

**$L^2$ inversion.** For $f \in L^2(\mathbb{R})$, the inversion holds in the $L^2$ sense:

$$
f = \lim_{R \to \infty} \int_{-R}^{R} \hat{f}(\xi) e^{2\pi i \xi x} \, d\xi,
$$

where the limit is taken in $L^2$ norm. This follows directly from $\mathcal{F}$ being unitary: $\mathcal{F}^{-1} = \mathcal{F}^*$ (the adjoint), and for this convention $\mathcal{F}^*\lbrace g\rbrace(x) = \int g(\xi) e^{2\pi i \xi x} d\xi$.

## Key Properties Table

| Property | Formula |
|---|---|
| Linearity | $\mathcal{F}\lbrace\alpha f + \beta g\rbrace = \alpha \hat{f} + \beta \hat{g}$ |
| Translation | $\mathcal{F}\lbrace f(x-a)\rbrace(\xi) = e^{-2\pi i a \xi} \hat{f}(\xi)$ |
| Modulation | $\mathcal{F}\lbrace e^{2\pi i \xi_0 x} f(x)\rbrace(\xi) = \hat{f}(\xi - \xi_0)$ |
| Dilation | $\mathcal{F}\lbrace f(ax)\rbrace(\xi) = (1/|a|)\hat{f}(\xi/a)$ |
| Differentiation | $\mathcal{F}\lbrace f'\rbrace(\xi) = 2\pi i \xi \, \hat{f}(\xi)$ |
| Convolution | $\mathcal{F}\lbrace f * g\rbrace = \hat{f} \cdot \hat{g}$ |

Each property is derived from direct substitution into the definition. The convolution theorem, for example, converts the integral operator $(f*g)(x) = \int f(x-y)g(y)dy$ into pointwise multiplication in the frequency domain — the fundamental reason why the Fourier transform simplifies linear time-invariant systems.

## Application: Solving the Heat Equation

The one-dimensional heat equation with thermal diffusivity $\kappa > 0$ reads

$$
\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \kappa \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}, \quad u(x,0) = u_0(x), \quad x \in \mathbb{R}, \; t > 0.
$$

Applying the Fourier transform in $x$ and using the differentiation property:

$$
\frac{\partial \hat{u}}{\partial t} = \kappa (2\pi i \xi)^2 \hat{u} = -4\pi^2 \kappa \xi^2 \hat{u}.
$$

This is an ODE in $t$ with solution

$$
\hat{u}(\xi, t) = \hat{u}_0(\xi) \, e^{-4\pi^2 \kappa \xi^2 t}.
$$

By the convolution theorem, $u(x,t) = u_0 * G_t$, where $G_t$ is the **heat kernel** (Gaussian):

$$
\boxed{G_t(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi \kappa t}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{x^2}{4\kappa t}\right).}
$$

This is the fundamental solution: the temperature profile at time $t$ is obtained by convolving the initial condition with a spreading Gaussian whose variance $\sigma^2 = 2\kappa t$ grows linearly with time. The Fourier transform reduces the PDE to an algebraic multiplication in frequency space — the paradigmatic application of transform methods to linear PDEs.

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def heat_kernel(x: np.ndarray, t: float, kappa: float) -> np.ndarray:
    """
    Evaluate the 1D heat kernel G_t(x).
    
    Args:
        x:     Spatial coordinates [m]
        t:     Time [s], must be > 0
        kappa: Thermal diffusivity [m^2/s]
    
    Returns:
        G_t(x): Heat kernel values
    """
    return np.exp(-x**2 / (4 * kappa * t)) / np.sqrt(4 * np.pi * kappa * t)

# Solve heat equation for a delta-function initial condition
x = np.linspace(-5, 5, 1000)
kappa = 0.01  # m^2/s (e.g. copper: 1.17e-4)
times = [0.1, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0]  # seconds

for t in times:
    plt.plot(x, heat_kernel(x, t, kappa), label=f"t = {t} s")

plt.xlabel("x [m]")
plt.ylabel("G_t(x) [1/m]")
plt.title("Heat kernel evolution")
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.show()
```
