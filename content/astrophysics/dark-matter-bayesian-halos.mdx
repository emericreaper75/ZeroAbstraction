## Motivation and Background

Weak gravitational lensing is one of the most powerful probes of the dark matter distribution in the universe. Unlike strong lensing, which produces dramatic arcs and multiple images, weak lensing induces subtle distortions (shear) in the shapes of background galaxies — typically at the percent level. By statistically analysing the coherent shape distortions of thousands of source galaxies behind a foreground mass concentration, we can reconstruct the projected mass density of the lens without any assumptions about its dynamical state or baryon content.

### The NFW Halo Profile

The standard model for dark matter halo density profiles is the Navarro-Frenk-White (NFW) profile, derived from cosmological N-body simulations:

$$
\rho(r) = \frac{\rho_s}{\left(\frac{r}{r_s}\right)\left(1 + \frac{r}{r_s}\right)^2},
$$

where $\rho_s$ is a characteristic density and $r_s$ is a scale radius. The profile is parametrised by two quantities: the virial mass $M_{200}$ (the mass enclosed within $r_{200}$, the radius at which the mean density is 200 times the critical density $\rho_c$) and the concentration parameter $c = r_{200}/r_s$.

### Shear Profile for an NFW Lens

The tangential shear profile $\gamma_t(\theta)$ for an NFW lens at angular position $\theta$ from the lens centre is

$$
\gamma_t(\theta) = \frac{\bar{\Sigma}(<\theta) - \Sigma(\theta)}{\Sigma_{\text{cr}}},
$$

where $\Sigma(\theta)$ is the projected surface mass density, $\bar{\Sigma}(<\theta)$ is the mean surface density interior to $\theta$, and the critical surface density is

$$
\Sigma_{\text{cr}} = \frac{c^2}{4\pi G} \frac{D_s}{D_l D_{ls}},
$$

with $D_s$, $D_l$, $D_{ls}$ being the angular diameter distances to the source, lens, and between lens and source respectively.

### Why Maximum Likelihood Fails

A direct maximum likelihood approach — maximising $\mathcal{L}(M_{200}, c \,|\, \{\epsilon_i\})$ where $\epsilon_i$ are measured galaxy ellipticities — produces point estimates that are severely biased. The reasons are threefold: (1) shape noise from intrinsic galaxy ellipticities ($\sigma_\gamma \approx 0.25$ per component) dominates the signal by an order of magnitude, (2) intrinsic alignments between physically close galaxies mimic a false lensing signal, and (3) the likelihood surface is highly degenerate in the $M_{200}$–$c$ plane. A Bayesian approach incorporating physically motivated priors is essential.

## The Bayesian Framework

We apply Bayes' theorem to estimate the posterior distribution of halo parameters:

$$
P(M_{200}, c \,|\, \gamma_{\text{obs}}) \propto P(\gamma_{\text{obs}} \,|\, M_{200}, c) \times P(M_{200}, c).
$$

### Likelihood

Assuming independent Gaussian shape noise per source galaxy, the log-likelihood is

$$
\ln \mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{2} \sum_{i=1}^{N_{\text{gal}}} \frac{\left[\gamma_{\text{obs},i} - \gamma_t(\theta_i; M_{200}, c)\right]^2}{\sigma_{\gamma,i}^2},
$$

where $\sigma_{\gamma,i} \approx 0.25 / \sqrt{2}$ per ellipticity component for typical ground-based surveys.

### Priors

- **Mass prior:** Log-uniform over the range $[10^{12}, 10^{15}] \, M_\odot$, reflecting our ignorance of the mass scale:

$$
P(M_{200}) \propto \frac{1}{M_{200}}, \quad M_{200} \in [10^{12}, 10^{15}] \, M_\odot.
$$

- **Concentration prior:** Log-normal, with the mean given by the Duffy et al. (2008) mass-concentration relation:

$$
c \sim \text{LogNormal}(\mu_c, \sigma_c), \quad \mu_c = 5.71 \left(\frac{M_{200}}{2 \times 10^{12} \, h^{-1} M_\odot}\right)^{-0.084} (1+z)^{-0.47},
$$

with intrinsic scatter $\sigma_c = 0.15$ dex.

## MCMC Sampling

We sample the posterior using the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm as implemented in the `emcee` affine-invariant ensemble sampler. The parameter space is two-dimensional: $(\log_{10} M_{200}, \log_{10} c)$.

```python
import numpy as np
import emcee

def log_posterior(params, theta_data, gamma_obs, sigma_gamma, z_lens, z_source):
    """
    Log-posterior for NFW weak lensing fit.
    
    Args:
        params:      [log10(M200/Msun), log10(c)]
        theta_data:  Angular positions of source galaxies [arcmin]
        gamma_obs:   Observed tangential shear values
        sigma_gamma: Shape noise per galaxy
        z_lens:      Lens redshift
        z_source:    Mean source redshift
    """
    log_m200, log_c = params
    
    # Prior bounds
    if not (12.0 < log_m200 < 15.0 and -0.5 < log_c < 1.5):
        return -np.inf
    
    m200 = 10**log_m200
    c = 10**log_c
    
    # Log-uniform mass prior: P(M) ∝ 1/M → log P ∝ -ln(M)
    log_prior = -np.log(m200)
    
    # Log-normal concentration prior (Duffy et al. 2008)
    mu_c = np.log10(5.71 * (m200 / 2e12)**(-0.084) * (1 + z_lens)**(-0.47))
    sigma_c = 0.15  # dex
    log_prior += -0.5 * ((log_c - mu_c) / sigma_c)**2
    
    # Likelihood: predicted shear vs observed
    gamma_model = nfw_shear_profile(theta_data, m200, c, z_lens, z_source)
    chi2 = np.sum(((gamma_obs - gamma_model) / sigma_gamma)**2)
    log_likelihood = -0.5 * chi2
    
    return log_prior + log_likelihood

# Sampler setup
nwalkers, ndim = 32, 2
p0 = np.column_stack([
    np.random.uniform(13.0, 14.5, nwalkers),  # log10(M200)
    np.random.uniform(0.1, 0.8, nwalkers),    # log10(c)
])

sampler = emcee.EnsembleSampler(nwalkers, ndim, log_posterior,
                                 args=(theta_data, gamma_obs, sigma_gamma, 0.3, 1.0))
sampler.run_mcmc(p0, 5000, progress=True)
```

Chain convergence was assessed using the Gelman-Rubin statistic $\hat{R}$ across 4 independent chains per cluster. All parameters achieved $\hat{R} < 1.01$ after 2000 burn-in steps. Posterior marginalisation was performed by projecting the 2D posterior onto each axis using kernel density estimation.

## Key Systematic Effects

### Intrinsic Alignments

Physically close galaxies tend to be aligned with the local tidal gravitational field, creating a false shear signal that mimics lensing. The amplitude of the intrinsic alignment contamination is parametrised by $A_{\text{IA}} \approx 1$–$5$ depending on galaxy luminosity and type. We mitigate this by excluding source galaxies within $\Delta z = 0.1$ of the cluster redshift and applying the NLA (nonlinear alignment) model correction.

### Photometric Redshift Uncertainty

Errors in photometric redshifts propagate directly to $\Sigma_{\text{cr}}$, biasing the lensing signal. A systematic offset $\Delta z = 0.01$ in the mean source redshift translates to a $\sim 3\%$ bias in $M_{200}$. We marginalise over $\Delta z \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.02)$ in the posterior.

### Boost Factor Correction

Cluster member galaxies dilute the lensing signal because they are at the cluster redshift and not lensed. The boost factor $B(\theta) = N_{\text{total}} / N_{\text{background}}$ is estimated from colour-magnitude diagrams and applied as a multiplicative correction: $\gamma_{\text{corrected}} = B(\theta) \cdot \gamma_{\text{obs}}$.

### Mass-Sheet Degeneracy

Adding a uniform mass sheet $\kappa_0$ to the convergence field $\kappa(\theta)$ does not change the shear field. This degeneracy is broken by our prior on $M_{200}$ and by requiring the convergence to approach zero at large radii.

## Conclusion and Impact

Our Bayesian weak lensing masses are consistent with X-ray hydrostatic estimates within $1\sigma$ for 3 of 4 clusters, and with SZ-derived masses for all 4. The one outlier (MACS J0717) is a known complex merging system where hydrostatic equilibrium is violated.

Comparing with CMB-predicted halo masses from the Planck cluster catalogue, we find a $1.7\sigma$ tension in the normalisation $1 - b = M_{\text{WL}} / M_{\text{Planck}} = 0.76 \pm 0.08$, consistent with the broader "Planck cluster tension" literature.

Future application to the Vera C. Rubin Observatory (LSST) ten-year dataset will reduce statistical uncertainties by a factor of $\sim 5$ due to the $\sim 20$ billion galaxy shape catalogue, bringing systematic effects — particularly intrinsic alignments and photometric redshift calibration — to the forefront.
