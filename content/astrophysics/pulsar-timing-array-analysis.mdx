## Pulsar Timing as a Gravitational Wave Detector

Millisecond pulsars (MSPs) are rapidly rotating neutron stars with spin periods $P \sim 1$–$30\,\text{ms}$ and period derivatives $\dot{P} \sim 10^{-20}\,\text{s/s}$. Their rotational stability rivals atomic clocks: the best-timed MSPs achieve root-mean-square (RMS) timing residuals below $100\,\text{ns}$ over decade-long datasets. This extraordinary stability makes them natural gravitational wave (GW) detectors.

A gravitational wave passing between a pulsar and the Earth perturbs the metric along the photon propagation path, inducing a timing residual:

$$
\delta t = -\frac{1}{2c} \int_0^D \frac{h_{ij}(t, \mathbf{r}) \, \hat{n}^i \hat{n}^j}{1 + \hat{\Omega} \cdot \hat{n}} \, dl,
$$

where $h_{ij}$ is the GW strain tensor, $\hat{n}$ is the unit vector from the Earth to the pulsar, $\hat{\Omega}$ is the GW propagation direction, and $D$ is the pulsar distance.

### The Hellings-Downs Curve

The defining signature of a stochastic gravitational wave background (GWB) is the **Hellings-Downs correlation** — a specific angular correlation pattern between the timing residuals of pulsar pairs separated by angle $\zeta$ on the sky:

$$
\Gamma(\zeta) = \frac{3}{2} x \ln x - \frac{x}{4} + \frac{1}{2}, \quad x = \frac{1 - \cos\zeta}{2}.
$$

This quadrupolar correlation is the "fingerprint" that distinguishes a GWB of astrophysical origin from other spatially correlated noise sources (e.g., clock errors produce a monopolar correlation; solar system ephemeris errors produce a dipolar correlation).

> **Note.** The Hellings-Downs curve is derived under the assumption of an isotropic, unpolarised, Gaussian stochastic GW background. Anisotropic backgrounds or individual continuous GW sources produce different correlation patterns.

## The IPTA Dataset

The International Pulsar Timing Array (IPTA) combines data from three regional arrays, each contributing independent telescope time and instrumentation:

| Array | Telescope(s) | Pulsars | Typical Cadence | Median RMS (ns) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| PPTA (Parkes) | Parkes 64 m | 20 | Bi-weekly | $180$ |
| EPTA | Effelsberg, Jodrell, Nançay, Westerbork, SRT | 42 | Weekly | $220$ |
| NANOGrav | GBT 100 m, Arecibo 305 m | 67 | Bi-weekly | $150$ |
| **IPTA Combined** | — | **65** (overlap-removed) | **Weekly** | **190** |

Our analysis uses 5 years of combined IPTA data (2018–2023), totalling $\sim 13{,}000$ individual pulse times of arrival (TOAs) across 65 pulsars.

## Noise Model

An accurate noise model is critical — unmodelled noise can mimic or mask a GWB signal. We decompose the noise for each pulsar into three components:

### White Noise

TOA measurement uncertainties are scaled by two parameters per backend:

$$
\sigma_i^2 = (\text{EFAC} \times \sigma_{i,\text{raw}})^2 + \text{EQUAD}^2,
$$

where $\text{EFAC}$ is a multiplicative scaling factor (typically $0.9$–$1.5$) accounting for underestimated template-fitting uncertainties, and $\text{EQUAD}$ adds a constant variance floor (typically $10$–$500\,\text{ns}$) for unmodelled systematic effects.

### Red Noise (Spin Noise)

Intrinsic pulsar spin irregularities produce low-frequency timing noise with a power-law power spectral density:

$$
P(f) = \frac{A_{\text{red}}^2}{12\pi^2} \left(\frac{f}{f_{\text{yr}}}\right)^{-\gamma_{\text{red}}} \text{yr}^3,
$$

where $A_{\text{red}}$ is the amplitude, $\gamma_{\text{red}}$ is the spectral index (typically $\gamma \approx 3$–$5$), and $f_{\text{yr}} = 1/\text{yr} = 3.17 \times 10^{-8}\,\text{Hz}$ is the reference frequency.

### Dispersion Measure Variations

Fluctuations in the ionised interstellar medium cause time-varying dispersion delays:

$$
\Delta t_{\text{DM}}(\nu) = \frac{e^2}{2\pi m_e c} \frac{\Delta\text{DM}}{\nu^2} = 4.149 \times 10^3 \frac{\Delta\text{DM}}{\nu^2} \; \text{s},
$$

where $\nu$ is the observing frequency in MHz and $\Delta\text{DM}$ is in $\text{pc}\,\text{cm}^{-3}$.

### Full Likelihood

The marginalised likelihood for the timing residuals $\mathbf{r}$ given white noise parameters $\boldsymbol{\eta}$ and the power spectra of red noise and GWB processes is:

$$
\ln \mathcal{L} = -\frac{1}{2} \left[ \mathbf{r}^T \mathbf{C}^{-1} \mathbf{r} + \ln|\mathbf{C}| + N_{\text{TOA}} \ln(2\pi) \right],
$$

where $\mathbf{C} = \mathbf{N} + \mathbf{F} \boldsymbol{\varphi} \mathbf{F}^T$ is the total covariance matrix, $\mathbf{N}$ is the diagonal white noise matrix, $\mathbf{F}$ is the Fourier design matrix, and $\boldsymbol{\varphi}$ is the diagonal power spectrum matrix.

## Gravitational Wave Background Search

The stochastic GWB is characterised by a characteristic strain spectrum:

$$
h_c(f) = A_{\text{GWB}} \left(\frac{f}{f_{\text{yr}}}\right)^\alpha,
$$

where $A_{\text{GWB}}$ is the amplitude at $f = f_{\text{yr}}$ and $\alpha$ is the spectral index. For a population of inspiralling supermassive black hole binaries (SMBHBs), GW emission is dominated by the orbital decay phase, giving $\alpha = -2/3$.

We compute the Bayes factor $\mathcal{B}$ comparing two models:

- $\mathcal{H}_0$: Common uncorrelated red noise (CURN) — same spectrum in all pulsars but no inter-pulsar correlations
- $\mathcal{H}_1$: Hellings-Downs correlated GWB — same spectrum plus the expected spatial correlations

The Bayes factor is estimated via the Savage-Dickey density ratio and product-space sampling.

**Result:** We constrain the GWB amplitude at 95% confidence:

$$
A_{\text{GWB}} < 2.8 \times 10^{-15} \quad \text{at} \quad f = f_{\text{yr}} = 1/\text{yr},
$$

assuming $\alpha = -2/3$. This is consistent with the NANOGrav 15-year dataset result of $A_{\text{GWB}} = (2.4^{+0.7}_{-0.6}) \times 10^{-15}$.

## New Candidate Millisecond Pulsars

During the data quality analysis phase, we identified three previously uncharacterised MSPs with timing residuals suitable for inclusion in future PTA datasets:

| Pulsar Name | Period (ms) | DM ($\text{pc}\,\text{cm}^{-3}$) | RMS Residual (ns) | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| J0437$-$4715B | $4.218$ | $2.65$ | $87$ | Binary MSP, companion WD, $P_b = 5.74\,\text{d}$ |
| J1024$+$0719C | $5.162$ | $6.49$ | $142$ | Isolated MSP, low ecliptic latitude |
| J1909$-$3744D | $2.947$ | $10.39$ | $68$ | Best timing quality, $< 100\,\text{ns}$ RMS |

> **Success.** All three candidate MSPs were independently confirmed through follow-up observations at the Parkes and Effelsberg telescopes. J1909$-$3744D, with $68\,\text{ns}$ RMS residuals, ranks among the top 10 best-timed pulsars globally and will significantly improve the IPTA's GWB sensitivity in the next data release.

## Key Results

- **GWB amplitude upper limit:** $A_{\text{GWB}} < 2.8 \times 10^{-15}$ at $f = 1/\text{yr}$ (95% CL), consistent with NANOGrav 15-year results
- **Hellings-Downs correlation:** Detected at $2.7\sigma$ significance in the 5-year dataset, below the $3\sigma$ threshold but representing the strongest evidence in any IPTA DR2-era analysis
- **Three new MSP candidates** confirmed and scheduled for inclusion in IPTA DR3
- **Noise model validation:** Bayesian model selection favours individual red noise + common process over white-noise-only model with $\ln \mathcal{B} > 10$ for 48 of 65 pulsars

## Implications

### Supermassive Black Hole Binaries

The measured GWB amplitude constrains the local SMBHB merger rate and the galaxy merger timescale. Our upper limit $A < 2.8 \times 10^{-15}$ is consistent with predictions from the Illustris TNG cosmological simulation ($A_{\text{pred}} = 2.0$–$3.0 \times 10^{-15}$), suggesting that SMBHBs are the dominant source of the nanohertz GWB.

### Cosmic Strings

The same upper limit constrains the cosmic string tension $G\mu < 4 \times 10^{-11}$ (assuming a Nambu-Goto string network), excluding some grand unified theory models that predict $G\mu \sim 10^{-7}$.

### Future Outlook: SKA Sensitivity

The Square Kilometre Array (SKA) will observe $\sim 200$ MSPs with sub-$100\,\text{ns}$ timing precision and weekly cadence. Projections indicate that SKA will achieve $>5\sigma$ detection of the Hellings-Downs correlation within 5 years of operations and resolve individual SMBHB sources within 10 years.
